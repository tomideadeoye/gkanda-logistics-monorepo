## Executive Statement

Safety, environmental protection, and legal compliance take precedence over production. This Plan is compulsory. Any deviation without prior written approval from the NPA Safety Director and the GK & A Managing Director is a breach subject to disciplinary action, removal from site, and contract termination for cause.

---

## Introduction

This Plan is a jointly endorsed, auditable operational control developed under Nigerian Ports Authority (NPA) oversight. Procedures, notification chains, and role interfaces have been reviewed with NPA and are mandatory for all personnel and contractors on GK & A-controlled areas of the NPA Lighter Terminal, Ikorodu.

**Enforcement**: Roles and accountabilities are assigned in writing. Compliance is continuously monitored. Violations are escalated through disciplinary protocols and may trigger regulatory reporting to NPA and relevant authorities.

**Joint Execution**: GK & A and NPA Safety Officers shall co-lead emergency activations, joint drills, audits, debriefs, and corrective actions. NPA retains authority to intervene and direct operations where necessary to protect life, environment, and port security.

---

## 1. Purpose

This Plan establishes an integrated, zero-harm emergency management framework to:

- Protect people, property, cargo integrity, and the environment.
- Prevent escalation and minimize business interruption.
- Achieve and evidence compliance with: NPA directives; ISPS Code; IMDG Code; ISO 45001 and ISO 14001 principles; ILO/IMO Code of Practice for Safety and Health in Ports; Lagos State and Federal environmental obligations.
- Apply the most stringent requirement when standards overlap.

**Measurable Objectives**:

- Zero fatalities; zero serious injuries (life-altering).
- External notifications initiated within 5 minutes of emergency declaration.
- Full headcount reconciliation within 10 minutes of evacuation order.
- Drill participation ≥ 90% of on-duty personnel; corrective actions closed ≤ 30 days.
- No uncontrolled discharge to water; all spills contained within the site boundary.

---

## 2. Scope

Applies 24/7 to GK & A-controlled operations within the NPA Lighter Terminal, Ikorodu:

- Quay, jetties, cargo yards, and operational zones under NPA regulation.
- Warehouses and open storage areas.
- Reefer container stacks, plug-in racks, and associated power/refrigeration systems.

**Interfaces and Inclusions**:

- Covers all employees, contractors, subcontractors, visitors, clients, and third-party carriers.
- Includes tenants or third parties while in GK & A-controlled zones.
- NPA Safety Officers participate in activations, drills, debriefs, and reviews.

**Exclusions**:

- Non-GK & A premises beyond defined interface points; however, GK & A shall support NPA-directed mutual aid and information sharing as requested.

## 3. Definitions

This Plan uses specific terminology to ensure clear communication and understanding among all personnel. An emergency is defined as any unplanned event that threatens life, the environment, assets, cargo integrity, or operational continuity. The Incident Commander (IC) serves as the on-scene authority with full responsibility for declaring emergencies, managing incident response, and approving re-entry to affected areas. A Deputy IC provides authorized backup command capability when the primary IC is unavailable or needs assistance.

The Emergency Response Team (ERT) consists of trained specialists organized into functional groups including Fire, HAZMAT/Spill, Medical, Refrigerant/Technical, and Security responders. The Public Information Officer (PIO) acts as the sole authorized spokesperson for all external communications, ensuring consistent and controlled messaging. Muster points are designated safe assembly locations where personnel gather for headcount verification following evacuation orders.

An exclusion zone represents a controlled perimeter that restricts access to only trained and authorized personnel during incident response. The ALARP principle (As Low As Reasonably Practicable) establishes the risk threshold for conducting operations and rescue activities. Refrigerants refer to substances used in refrigerated containers, including A1 classifications such as R-134a, R-404A, and R-452A, as well as any A2L mildly flammable refrigerants that may be present.

Shelter-in-place procedures involve controlled indoor protection measures during external airborne threats or security incidents. Command Posts (CP) include both primary and secondary locations where the IC and key staff coordinate incident response activities. Staging areas serve as controlled locations where emergency resources and response teams assemble before being assigned to specific tasks.

## 6. Governance, Command, and Responsibilities

### 6.1 Command and Control

The incident management framework follows a structured Incident Command System (ICS) approach that scales according to incident complexity. The IC leads the overall response with support from specialized sections including Operations, Planning, Logistics, and Finance/Administration. A dedicated Safety Officer ensures ongoing risk assessment, while the PIO manages all external communications. An NPA Liaison Officer coordinates with port authorities and ensures regulatory compliance throughout the response.

Command Posts are strategically located to provide optimal oversight and safety. The primary Command Post serves as the main coordination center, while a secondary Command Post positioned upwind or up-current provides backup capability if the primary location becomes compromised. A designated Media Area ensures controlled access for press and external stakeholders during major incidents.

Staging Areas are established as controlled locations where emergency resources and response teams assemble before assignment to specific tasks. Both primary and secondary staging points are predefined to ensure efficient resource deployment and team coordination.

### 6.2 Roles and Authorities

Clear role definitions and authority levels ensure effective emergency response coordination. The Managing Director holds ultimate accountability, providing necessary resources, approving Plan revisions, chairing the GK & A side of the Joint Emergency Steering Committee, and authorizing escalations and capital expenditures for life-safety improvements. The NPA Safety Director co-chairs governance activities, endorses Plan revisions, and ensures alignment with NPA requirements and standards.

The Incident Commander serves as the on-scene authority with full decision-making power, declaring emergencies, establishing response priorities, approving re-entry to affected areas, ordering evacuations and search-and-rescue operations, and authorizing hot work through permit systems after incident stabilization. The Deputy IC provides command continuity and assumes the IC role when required.

The NPA Liaison Officer coordinates with NPA Port Control, Port Police, and Marine units while ensuring all statutory notifications reach appropriate agencies. ERT Leaders for Fire, HAZMAT, Medical, Security, and Technical functions task and supervise their teams, ensure proper personal protective equipment (PPE) usage and gas monitoring, and provide regular status updates to the IC.

The Warehouse Controller manages emergency shutdown procedures, secures hazardous storage areas, and supports personnel accountability processes. The Reefer Maintenance Lead oversees reefer power isolation and restoration, coordinates with certified refrigeration contractors, and protects temperature-critical cargo throughout the incident.

The Security Supervisor establishes and maintains incident perimeters, manages access control and gate protocols, preserves evidence for investigations, and coordinates with NPA Port Police. The PIO controls all public, media, and customer messaging, issues holding statements during active incidents, maintains rumor control, and enforces social media communication policies.

All personnel and contractors share fundamental responsibilities including raising alarms when emergencies are detected, stopping work immediately, evacuating when ordered, following IC and ERT instructions, participating in drills and training, and supporting headcount procedures. Non-compliance with these requirements results in immediate removal from site and disciplinary action up to contract termination.

### 6.3 Minimum Staffing and Duty Rosters

Emergency response capability requires continuous staffing coverage to ensure immediate response to any incident. A minimum of three fire responders, two HAZMAT specialists, two medical responders, two security personnel, and one technical specialist must be on duty at all times. The Incident Commander or Deputy IC must be accessible within ten minutes of any emergency declaration. Detailed duty rosters are documented on a weekly basis to ensure coverage meets these minimum requirements.

Competency maintenance is critical for response effectiveness. All on-duty ERT members must maintain current certification in self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) and gas monitoring equipment within the past 12 months. First aid responders must maintain automated external defibrillator (AED) competency within the past 24 months.

### 6.4 Business Continuity Linkage

Emergency response planning integrates closely with broader business continuity management. The Plan connects directly with the GK & A Business Continuity Plan (BCP) to ensure comprehensive protection of critical operations. This includes maintaining reefer container continuity through backup power systems, identifying alternate cold storage facilities, establishing mutual aid agreements for reefer power support, and maintaining clear communication protocols with clients during prolonged operational outages.

## 7. Activation, Notification, and Initial Actions

### 7.1 Triggers

- Fire/explosion/smoke alarm; chemical or fuel spill; refrigerant alarm/odor; serious injury; security incident; severe weather/flooding/lightning; structural compromise; person overboard; extensive power loss affecting reefers.

### 7.2 First Five Minutes (Mandatory)

- Raise alarm: pull station or radio in clear text "EMERGENCY – [Location] – [Type]."
- IC or senior-most present declares emergency; stands up Command Post; assigns scribe; opens Incident Log with time-stamps.
- Dispatch ERT; confirm PPE (SCBA for unknown atmospheres).
- Notify NPA Port Control immediately; escalate to Fire/EMS/Police/LASEMA/LASWA/NIWA as needed per matrix.
- Lock down or open gates as appropriate to incident type to secure site or facilitate emergency ingress.

### 7.3 First Ten Minutes

- Order evacuation or shelter-in-place as conditions dictate.
- Establish exclusion zones; deny entry.
- Achieve full headcount reconciliation at muster points within 10 minutes; identify missing persons; do not start rescue unless risk is ALARP and approved by IC.
- Issue SITREP 1 to leadership and NPA.

### 7.4 Alerting Systems

- Site sirens/PA; UHF radios (Channels: 1 Ops, 2 ERT, 3 Command); SMS/app alerts. Backups: air horns, runners.

## 8. Emergency Response Procedures

Emergency response procedures follow a structured hierarchy of priorities: protect life first, then isolate hazards, prevent escalation, protect the environment, and preserve evidence for investigation. Each incident type requires specific protocols while maintaining these core principles.

### 8.1 Fire

Fire response requires immediate protective measures and systematic attack based on the fire type and location. Responders must don full structural firefighting gear and self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) for interior or close approaches to ensure personal safety. Electrical and reefer container fires demand power isolation as the first action when safely possible, followed by application of carbon dioxide or dry chemical powder extinguishers while avoiding water on live electrical components.

Flammable liquid fires require foam application with careful attention to drainage protection and boom deployment to contain runoff. Fluorinated foams should be avoided unless no alternative exists, with preference given to fluorine-free F3 foam formulations. Warehouse and container stack fires necessitate a complete 360-degree size-up, exposure protection, collapse zone establishment, thermal imaging camera use to confirm fire knockdown, and cautious ventilation to prevent flashover. Compartment doors remain closed until the area is fully cooled and monitored.

Post-fire procedures include a minimum 60-minute fire watch period, containment and sampling of water runoff, and comprehensive documentation of all response actions and foam usage.

### 8.2 Hazardous Spills/HAZMAT

HAZMAT spill response begins with safe approach techniques, approaching upwind and upstream while identifying materials through Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) references. Chemical-resistant personal protective equipment (PPE) is mandatory, with SCBA consideration for unknown or toxic atmospheres.

Source control represents the immediate priority, including stopping the release, isolating ignition sources, and implementing grounding and bonding for flammable materials. Response includes deployment of drain covers, absorbent booms, absorbents, and temporary bunding to protect watercourses. Collection procedures involve overpacking materials in drums and labeled containers, waste segregation, and storage in designated HAZMAT areas pending licensed disposal.

Notifications must be made immediately to NPA Port Control and environmental authorities (LASEPA/NESREA) when regulatory thresholds are met, with accurate recording of spill volumes and materials. Decontamination procedures establish gross decon stations for responders and casualties when required before emergency medical services handover.

### 8.3 Refrigerant Leaks (Reefer Stacks)

Refrigerant leaks present multiple hazards including oxygen displacement from A1 refrigerants, cold burn injuries, and mild flammability with ignition risks from A2L refrigerants. Decomposition products may also form if materials are heated.

Immediate control measures include evacuation of the affected area, establishment of no-ignition zones, and minimum 50-meter cordons when concentration and wind conditions are unknown. Cordon distances adjust based on monitoring readings. Hot work prohibition and electrical power isolation to affected units prevent ignition sources.

Monitoring utilizes calibrated refrigerant leak detectors (NDIR/IR for halocarbons) combined with multi-gas meters. Alarm set-points warn at 10% lower flammable limit (LFL) for A2L refrigerants and trigger evacuation/cordoning at 20-25% LFL or higher. A1 refrigerants are managed as asphyxiants using SDS occupational exposure limits (typically 1,000 ppm time-weighted average).

Response involves natural or mechanical ventilation with continuous airflow maintenance in reefer alleys. Interlocked hot work shutdown systems activate where fixed detection exists. Only qualified technicians perform isolation and repair work, with refrigerant recovery to approved cylinders and detailed service record maintenance.

Re-occupation requires confirmation of safe atmospheric conditions: oxygen levels between 19.5-23.5%, lower explosive limit below 10%, refrigerant concentrations below SDS occupational exposure limits, and two consecutive stable readings taken 5-10 minutes apart.

### 8.4 Medical Emergencies

Medical emergency response prioritizes scene safety and rapid intervention. The incident is announced clearly as "Medical Emergency – General Hospital Ikorodu" while deploying automated external defibrillators (AEDs) and trauma kits. Assessment follows standard DRSABC protocols with immediate control of bleeding. Burns receive cooling treatment with clean water for at least 20 minutes, while chemical exposures require flushing for 15 minutes or more with SDS consultation.

Heat stress cases involve immediate removal from heat sources, active cooling measures, hydration, and vital sign monitoring. Coordination with Lagos State Ambulance Service (LASAMBUS) occurs through emergency numbers 112 or 767, with ambulance guidance through designated gates. Comprehensive casualty and witness logs are maintained, and all equipment receives decontamination after use.

### 8.5 Security Incidents

Security incidents trigger immediate lockdown and access control measures following Port Facility Security Plan (PFSP) protocols, including badge deactivation when required. Closed-circuit television (CCTV) preservation and evidence handling procedures activate automatically. Bomb threats and suspicious packages follow established checklists with upwind evacuation and 15-meter radio exclusion zones around suspect devices.

Intrusion and theft incidents prohibit pursuit of suspects while requiring scene preservation and immediate NPA Port Police notification. Civil disturbances and armed threats activate shelter-in-place procedures with safe room protocols, silent alert code words, and full law enforcement compliance. The Public Information Officer (PIO) manages all external communications, with strict prohibition of social media posting by staff members.

### 8.6 Severe Weather and Flooding

Severe weather response includes continuous monitoring of lightning alerts, wind thresholds, and tidal surge data. Immediate actions involve securing loose cargo and equipment, suspending high-risk work activities, and ceasing crane operations when wind speeds exceed defined safety limits. Vulnerable goods receive elevation, critical records are protected, and non-essential power systems shut down.

Stormwater controls activate with sandbag and boom deployment, and valved drains are isolated as needed. Reefer container continuity maintains priority through emergency power transfer following established prioritization matrices, with manual temperature checks and logging during outages. Post-event procedures include comprehensive structural, electrical, and environmental inspections with full documentation of findings and corrective actions.

### 8.7 Person Overboard

Person overboard incidents require immediate verbal alerts with repeated shouts of "Man Overboard" while maintaining constant visual contact and pointing at the casualty. Rescue begins with lifebuoy deployment equipped with lights and lines, followed by additional flotation devices and throw-bags. Nearby craft halt operations unless directed otherwise, with immediate notification to NPA Marine Police and launch of designated rescue vessels when safe.

Approach maneuvers occur leeward to avoid crush points between vessel and quay. Recovery procedures address hypothermia and aspiration risks with ambulance coordination, evidence preservation, and comprehensive debriefing sessions.

### 8.8 Confined Space Incident/Rescue

Confined space incidents prohibit entry without proper permits and rescue plans. Atmospheric testing occurs before any access, with continuous monitoring throughout the operation. Rescue activities are conducted exclusively by trained teams equipped with retrieval systems and standby personnel. Self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) or supplied air respiratory systems are used as required, with detailed entry and exit logs maintained for all activities.

### 8.9 Crane/Load Handling Incidents

Crane and load handling incidents require immediate establishment of collapse zones and area isolation while confirming structural stability. Loads remain unmoved until specialist assessment occurs, with rigging failure investigations and evidence preservation of all lifting gear. Recovery operations engage qualified specialists to ensure safe resolution.

### 8.10 Cyber Incident Impacting Safety/Operations

Cyber incidents affecting safety or operations trigger immediate incident response protocols with affected network isolation. Safety-critical systems transition to manual controls following Business Continuity Plan (BCP) procedures, particularly for reefer monitoring and access control systems. Paper-based logging systems activate to maintain operational records. Leadership and NPA receive immediate notification when port operations are impacted.

### 8.11 Utility Loss/Black Start

Utility loss events follow structured black start procedures beginning with generator readiness verification and automatic transfer switch (ATS) initiation or manual transfer. Priority load confirmation ensures reefer racks, control room, lighting, and communications receive power first. Load shedding occurs according to established matrices while fuel autonomy and quality receive verification through water-in-fuel testing. All times and load transfers are fully documented.

## 9. Evacuation, Shelter-in-Place, Accountability, and Re-entry

### 9.1 Evacuation

**Triggers**: Fire; HAZMAT/refrigerant; structural threat; person overboard in proximity; security per IC; severe weather.

**Routes and Muster Points**: Use primary/secondary routes to four muster points located upwind/up-current and away from reefer banks and HAZMAT stores; maps in Appendix B; muster capacity limits posted.

**Assisted Evacuation**: Buddy system; evacuation chairs where needed; Wardens/Marshals sweep zones, stairwells, amenities.

**Vehicles**: Do not use unless directed for controlled egress or to clear critical paths.

### 9.2 Shelter-in-Place

**Trigger**: External toxic plume; civil disturbance/armed threat; lightning with no safe egress.

**Actions**: Move indoors; close doors/windows; switch off ventilation; seal gaps; stay tuned to Channel 3; await all-clear.

### 9.3 Accountability

- Headcount completed within 10 minutes using T-cards, access logs, visitor registers, PTW lists; report missing persons with last known location; IC approves search-and-rescue only when risk is ALARP.

### 9.4 Re-entry and Re-energization

**IC grants re-entry with NPA concurrence after**:

- Atmosphere safe: O2 19.5–23.5%; LEL < 10%; CO < 25 ppm; H2S/other toxics below OEL per SDS; refrigerant below OEL per SDS; two consecutive stable readings 5–10 minutes apart.
- No visible smoke/heat; structural integrity confirmed; utilities safe; spills neutralized/contained; drains protected.

**Phased Re-entry**: Essential personnel first; full re-energization via permit-to-work and hot work permits as applicable.

**Post Re-entry**: 60-minute fire watch and status update to all staff/clients as needed.

Gas detection equipment includes eight multi-gas meters measuring oxygen, lower explosive limit, carbon monoxide, and hydrogen sulfide, with bump testing before each use or shift and quarterly calibration. ### C.6 Power Continuity

## Appendix G — Chemical and Refrigerant Inventory and SDS Index

### G.1 Inventory Fields

The chemical inventory system tracks comprehensive information for each material including identification details (material name, UN number, IMDG classification, packing group, and CAS number), storage parameters (location and maximum quantity), documentation (SDS version and date), health and safety data (occupational exposure limits for time-weighted average and short-term exposure, flammable limits, vapor density, water reactivity), detection requirements (compatible detector types), hazard controls (primary hazards, required personal protective equipment, first-aid measures, spill kit specifications), storage protocols (segregation requirements), and regulatory compliance (notification thresholds for environmental releases).

### G.2 Example Entries (Replace with Actuals)

Diesel fuel, classified as UN 1202 under IMDG Class 3 Packing Group III with CAS number 68334-30-5, is stored in the bunded fuel tank FT-1 located in the west yard. The maximum quantity allowed is to be determined based on site-specific assessments. Safety data sheets should be reviewed regularly with dates tracked. Occupational exposure limits are set at 5 mg/m³ for oil mists as a time-weighted average. Flammable limits range from approximately 0.6% to 7.5% by volume, with a vapor density greater than air. Detection requires lower explosive limit monitors using catalytic or infrared sensors. Personal protective equipment includes anti-static clothing, nitrile gloves, and safety goggles. Spill response requires oil-only spill kits, booms, drain covers, and foam extinguishers for fire control. Storage must be segregated away from oxidizing materials.

Hydraulic lubricating oil, typically not regulated as dangerous goods though SDS verification is required, is stored on workshop racks. Maximum quantities are determined by operational needs. Occupational exposure limits match diesel fuel at 5 mg/m³ for oil mists. Response requires oil-only spill kits for containment.

Battery electrolyte containing sulfuric acid is classified as UN 2796 under IMDG Class 8 Packing Group II with CAS number 7664-93-9. Storage occurs in the battery room within acid-resistant cabinets with bunding. Maximum quantities are established based on battery bank requirements. Occupational exposure limits are 0.2 ppm as a short-term exposure limit, verified through SDS review. Detection uses acid gas monitors. Personal protective equipment includes acid-resistant gloves, apron, and face shield. Neutralization requires sodium bicarbonate and copious water. Storage must be segregated away from bases and metals. Environmental releases exceeding threshold quantities require immediate notification to LASEPA.

Flammable paints and solvents, typically classified as UN 1263 under IMDG Class 3 with varying packing groups, are stored in ventilated flame-proof paint lockers. Lower flammable limits range from approximately 1% to 1.4%. Control measures prohibit ignition sources and require bonding and grounding during transfer operations.

Alkaline cleaning chemicals and degreasers follow SDS-driven hazard assessments. Storage requires segregation from acids with nearby eyewash stations for emergency response.

Refrigerant gases include R-134a (A1 classification, non-flammable but asphyxiant at high concentrations), R-404A (A1), and R-452A (A1), all with occupational exposure limits around 1,000 ppm time-weighted average as verified by SDS. Storage occurs in ventilated cages for both in-use and spare cylinders. Detection uses infrared sensors. Where A2L refrigerants like R-1234yf or R-1234ze are present, they are mildly flammable requiring lower flammable limit monitoring, ventilation maintenance, and prohibition of ignition sources.

### G.3 SDS Management

A master safety data sheet index is maintained and made accessible to all staff. Individual SDS documents are positioned at point-of-use locations. Optional QR code linking to digital versions may be implemented. Annual reviews ensure currency and accuracy of all documentation.

### G.4 Segregation and Labeling

Chemical storage follows International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) segregation requirements by hazard class. Corrosive materials are stored separately from bases and flammables. Oxidizers are isolated in designated areas. All containers use Globally Harmonized System (GHS) labeling with secondary containers clearly marked. Storage rooms maintain ventilation systems with eyewash and safety shower stations positioned nearby for emergency access.

### G.5 Environmental Thresholds and Notifications

Reportable quantities for environmental releases are established in coordination with LASEPA and NESREA regulatory authorities. Any release to waterways requires immediate telephone notification followed by statutory written reports within prescribed timelines.

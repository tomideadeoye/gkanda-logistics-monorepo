3.1 Proposed Project Location

The proposed project site is located at Ipakodo / Ebute-LGA waterfront of the Lagos Lagoon, in Ikorodu Local Government Area of Lagos State and falls on the Geographical Coordinate: N 6° 36’ 22.75” and E3° 28’38.07”. The project site is located at an estimated 1km and 1.5km to the Nigeria Customs Service Ikorodu Lighter Terminal and Lagos State Ferry Terminal respectively. The site shares common land boundary with some residential property on the western, eastern and southern parts while the northern part is basically a Lagoon through which the shipment of containers will be made to the proposed terminal, Within the immediate vicinity on the western part of the proposed site, are Dangote Pasta, Flour and Noodles production facilities.

The proposed project area is a mixed land use comprising majorly of residential and commercial development. In addition to the Government Lighter terminal facility in proximity to the proposed project site, there also exist other similar container terminal facilities located at an estimated 5-6km away along Ibeshe waterfront of the project area. The proposed project has a life span of 50 years, and may be extended as the needs arises. Land Requirement

The proposed container terminal project will be developed on approximately 23.893 hectares of space consisting of dry land and water bodies. The existing dry land area of the site is less than 2 acres of land and the water body abutting this area will be reclaimed to achieve the required 20 hectares of land space for the development of the terminal as shown in the attached Survey Plan for the project. Part of the existing land area of the proposed site (Origin jetty) was formerly used as a passenger jetty before it was acquired by GK&A Logistics Services for the intended container development with all documentation with respect to the ownership of the land having being completed.

The assessment of the project site indicates that the proposed terminal can be developed within the purview of existing legal and regulatory framework for such infrastructure development. 3.2 Proposed Project Overview

The proposed project involves construction and operation of a bonded and EPT container terminal with design capacity to handle more than 10,000 TEUs of containers in transit at any given time. The proposed project is a private sector initiative aimed at assisting the Nigerian Ports Authority (NPA) in the provision of technical capacity and logistics support to the sea ports in Nigeria through the development of container holding terminal in a strategic location to help shippers move their container cargoes with ease and away from the persistent Apapa gridlock.

Shipping container cargo arriving from the point of first discharge at Apapa and Tin-Can Ports in Lagos shall be received in the proposed terminal using barge transportation on the Lagos Lagoon, The containers are then stored in the terminal until they are required to be moved to different destinations and end user places across the country via road trucks upon the completion of necessary assessment by relevant authorities including inspection and payment of customs duty at the terminal. The project is anticipated to improve the turn-around time for Vessels calling at the Nigerian Ports, as space will be freed up for more import activities.

The proposed terminal facility has additional capacity for function and growth because of the way it has been designed. The project when completed, is anticipated to contribute to a great extent to ameliorating problems of congestion (that have defied solutions) in Apapa ports. Dredging and Reclamation Activities Prior to commencement of any form of works on site, the site boundary shall be re-established to ensure that lands not acquired by the proponent are not encroached upon.

Dredging Cutter Suction Dredger (CSD) will be deployed for the proposed dredging and reclamation works. A cutter suction dredger cuts hard cuts hard soil into fragments with a rotating cutter head, The selection of Cutter suction dredge for the proposed dredging is based on the following factors: * Physical characteristics of material to be dredged; * Quantities of material to be dredged; * Depth of the sand winning area; * Distance to disposal area; * Physical environment of and between the dredging site and disposal area; * Contamination level of sediments; * Potential disposal alternative

It is also selected due to the fact that wave heights around the proposed sand winning area are not excessive compare to the situation on the far southern part of the Lagoon. Because it is equipped with a rotating cutter apparatus surrounding the intake end of the suction pipe, it can efficiently dig and pump all types of alluvial materials and compacted deposits, such as clay and hardpan.

This dredge has the capability of pumping dredged material long distances to upland disposal sites. The material is sucked up by dredge pumps and discharged to the reclamation area through pipelines. The equipment consist of pontoon, which is positioned with a spudpole at the back and two anchors at the front. The soil is loosened by rotating a cutting head: “the cutter”, while powerful inboard centrifugal pump(s) hydraulically transport the loosened soil. The cutterhead, which is hydraulically driven encloses the suction intake of centrifugal dredge pump, The cutterhead is mounted at the extremity of a fabricated steel structure, named the ” ladder” which attached to the main haul heavy hinges that permit rotation in the vertical plane. The ladder assembly is lowered and raised by means of a hoisting controlled from the bridge. By means of winches, the cutter ( and therefore the whole pontoon), is pulled in turns to the portside and starboard-side anchor whilst turning on the fixed spud-pole. In this way (part of) a circular movement is made whilst a so-called spud-pole carrier enables a forward movement of the dredger.

The vertical position of cutting head and the ladder can be measured by the any of the following ways: * The length of a thin wire connected from the ladder to the pontoon, through heaves etc. * Angle between the ladder and the pontoon in combination with draught and trim of the pontoon. * The depth of the cutting head can be determined by using pressure readings from specially installed sensors.

The above-mentioned measures make the CSD able to determine how deep and in what the cutting head is used for dredging. After loosening and suction, the soil is pumped into a floating pipeline and a land pipeline. The overall production of CSD depends on many factors, among which: soil characteristics such as situ density, particle distribution and SPT and/or CPT values, dredging depth, weather conditions, marine traffic, tides, currents, etc. The CSD operations continue 24 hours a day and 7 days a week. In addition to the CSD.other machinery and equipment that will be deployed during dredging include tugboat, multicat, workboat, rampbarge, fuel barge, spray pontoon, survey launch and local speedboat.

A TEU is unit for measuring container storage such that a space for two 20 foot containers is equivalent to one 40 footer. The apron width would be 60m, 5m of which would be used as the running lane of the yard trucks which load/unload containers when barges are moored alongside the berth. Berth/ Quay Wall The berths provide a vertical front which allows safe and secure mooring that facilitate the loading and unloading of container from the barge. The influence factors which effected on the structure were taken into consideration in the design of the proposed berth. This includes soil characteristics of the proposed location, environmental conditions and rage of traffic. The berth is expected to receive lateral loads from the berthing and mooring of barges. Berthing forces occur when a barge berths along a deck and transmits the force along the contact length of the fender system. This force is then distributed by the diaphragm actions to the pile bents and this is expected to be transmitted to the retained upland fill, directly to the fill. The proposed quay wall has an approximate total length of 120m and designed to allow three (3) standard barges to berth simultaneously.

Generally, the berth will be of a concrete type as this is more durable and has longer lifecycle than others materials. The berth/quay wall will be constructed using steel sheet piles wall being the most embedded retaining wall elements for container terminal. The piles will be driven to a considerable depth with low displacement and generally involve the following steps: i. Installation of cased bore piles; ii. Installation of deck PC elements from the floating pontoon; iii. Placement of deck reinforcement and cast in situ reinforcement concrete; iv. Demolition of top existing cap beam; v. Attachment of anchor wall and placement of tie rod; vi. Construction of capping beam cast and pavement slab;

Sheet Pile Driving: The equipment to be used for this purpose is hydraulic crane with hammer placed on a pontoon in the Lagoon and another on land. This involves driving the sheet piles into the ground to form the quay wall. During the pile driving, an attachment is placed on the pilling hammer to guide the driving direction. Also to guide the quay wall, concrete sheet piles are driven into the ground behind the steel sheet piles and both tied together by 40mm steel rods at 2m centers, and at 3m interval for strength and stability.

Capping Beam: The capping beam is an in-situ concrete casting to stabilise the steel sheet pile wall. The pile driving is followed by capping beam work which involve tieing with steel rods 40mm for reinforcement and stabilisation, after which the pre-cast capping elements will then be laid on top of the piles cast in-situ. In-situ concrete casting shall be used to complete the capping beam.

Mooring Bollard (Anchor) wall and mooring bollard (Anchor) installation: Mooring bollard wall shall be installed to connect the steel anchor with the capping beam. From both sides, the steel anchors shall be installed and bolted in the middle with two (2) threaded bars and nuts. This installation enhances safe anchoring of barges for subsequent container loading and unloading operations.

There is also a future plan to run the entire facility on renewable energy source so as to minimize emission associated climate change.

As part of security monitoring system, lighting system with high mast lighting tower will be installed around the terminal for illumination at night for 24 hours’ activities and for security purposes. Fuel Supply A large storage of diesel is not anticipated. Diesel will be received in trucks and stored in 500 or 1000 litres above ground and double-wall storage tank. The storage tank area will be surrounded by a concrete bund wall equivalent to the volume of the above ground double-wall storage tank.

Water supply Water supply will be from a borehole which will be drilled on site. Water shall be used for spraying the terminal ground, domestic purpose and fire-fighting and washing of terminal equipment. An estimated 30 gallons of water will be required for terminal operation on a daily basis.

Storm water drainage system The proposed container terminal will be equipped with storm water drainage network to drain the runoff generated from the facility premises into the surrounding water bodies. The drainage system will be a covered type and this consists of reinforced concrete which will be finished to a gentle gradient. Operational area storm water such as workshop and trucking areas will be collected into a coalescence pit, where the contaminated storm water will be treated before discharge into Lagoon.

Sewage Collection Facility A sewage storage tank will be constructed for the collection of human waste generated from the domestic activities and office related waste. All toilet facilities within the building and gate house will be channeled to the sewage facility from where before the waste is evacuated by LASWAMO accredited waste contractor.

Operations and services in the proposed container terminal shall be done in a safe, efficient, effective and economic manner, and in accordance with applicable regulations and generally accepted practices, methods, techniques and standards employed internationally with respect to the development, operation, maintenance, personnel and terminal safety and environment protection.

The equipment will be used mainly for loading and unloading operation. Forklift: The forklift is considered the most basic piece of intermodal equipment and will be used to handle 20-foot containers or empty containers of other dimensions only.

Terminal Operation Planning Generally, operations planning of the proposed container terminal typically, will involve coordinating the movement of six key elements which are: * Barge inbound and outbound * Quay crane discharging and loading systems * Internal transportation and container movement * Container yard discharging and loading system * Stacking system * Gate system; and * Inbound and outbound process for containers

Gate Operations All inbound and outbound trucks to the terminal will be through the main access gate. When a truck arrives at the terminal to deliver a container, the container is registered and quickly checked for damages. Also the seal is checked. It is expected that handling of a truck will take about 5 minutes at the gate. Then the truck driver is given a position within the container yard where he has to drive. At that position, the container is stacked into the storage block by and straddle carrier. If the truck is not supposed to pick up another container on the terminal, it leaves the terminal. Once the barge on which the outbound container to the (Apapa or Tin Island port) is supposed to leave the terminal, the container is removed from the storage block and moved to the quay where it is lifted onto the barge to the shipping for final export.

Import containers are lifted from the barge to UTRs by the STS-crane which then moves the container to the storage yard where it is stacked in by an RTG-crane. A container that is supposed to leave by truck is picked up by an external truck directly at the storage block, where it is loaded by RTG.

Barge Operations A barge is a flat-shaped on its bottom, just like a raft. They cannot move on their own but will be towed with a tugboat to/from the shipping port to/from the proposed terminal. Once a container laden barge arrive at the terminal docking area the containers are lifted onto the terminal trailers. The twist locks are removed manually right before the container is positioned on the trailer because roll flat type of trailers are used without notches at the corners. Here also the seal check, the container registration and a quick damage check is performed manually. Between two and three terminal tractors are used to serve the STS.

Container Storage Operations One of the key aspects in container terminal operation is the container-storage block- allocation. The choice of a position for storing a certain container strongly influences the performance of the terminal. The most basic strategy is to segregate stacks for inbound and outbound containers. Containers of different destination and type are stored in different areas. Lagos port inbound containers shall be stored next to the quay to reduce travel times between storage area and STS while the terminal inbound containers will be stored on the eastern side of the terminal in accordance with the project planning arrangement. The containers are stacked from 5 to 9 level heights using the reach-stackers. Stacking height is not limited by regulations but only by available equipment. The first in first out method (FIFO) will be adopted for stacking the container in order to avoid the prolong storage of container in the terminal.

Road Traffic Management It is anticipated that, the number of container trucks circulating along Oba Sekumade Road which the connecting road from the terminal location is expected to increase significantly over the life time of the project due to potential increase in terminal throughput. To this end, vehicular movement shall be organised in such a manner so as not to cause any obstruction to other road users. To this end, a call-up electronic system would be installed to streamline loading and unloading of containers. Basically, traffic control officers would be on hand to effectively take charge of vehicular movement in and around the terminal. Also, it is expected that the project will contribute to increase in the number of barges circulating in the navigation access channel over the lifetime of the project.

Repair and Maintenance There would be deployment of dedicated facility management team that ensures prompt repairs of all terminal facilities and structures. Maintenance operations will involve cleaning of terminal ground, repair and servicing of terminal facilities, handling equipment, generator, cracks on structures, building accessories, amongst others. The maintenance of container handling equipment will be carried at the designated workshop designed for this purpose. Maintenance and repair plan would be developed to organize specific maintenance and repair operations, the ways of implementation of those operations, and points of attention in such implementation, on the basis of the total assessment of checks and diagnosis.

Health Safety Security and Environment The proponent recognize that excellent Health Safety Security Environment (HSSE) performance is key to the success of business as port operators. The business activities within the proposed terminal premises will be conducted in a manner that minimizes adverse health, safety and environmental impacts with the aim of zero distress to people and the environment. On the part of environmental compliance, the proposed project will comply with all applicable Laws concerning the protection of the environment. 3.5.4 Decommissioning and Closure Phase

The proposed project is designed for a life span of 50- year life span. Decommissioning of the facility will be effected when the active life of all the facilities and structures have expired and will involve the removal of all the installed facilities and restoring the land where necessary. Facilities that shall be removed include: * Quay structure * Terminal platform * Container handling equipment * Administrative building * Utilities such as water and fuel storage tanks, concrete structures, storm water drainage channel

As part of the decommissioning plan, GK& A Logistics Services will develop decommissioning plans which will be submitted to Lagos State Ministry of the Environment and Water Resources and NPA.

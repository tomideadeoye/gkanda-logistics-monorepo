**4.8 Description of the Baseline Environment**

**Climate / Meteorology**

The meteorological and air quality analysis for the study area tends to underscore the state of atmospheric variables on the environment. Meteorological variables such as wind speeds and direction, relative humidity, temperature are crucial in the evaluation of pollutant dispersions and effluents from both anthropogenic and natural induced activities that take place in any environment. The atmosphere serves as sink or a medium of transfer through meteorological activities for these impacts. Rainfall is the key climatic variable and there are marked alternation of wet and dry seasons in most areas. Two air masses controls rainfall - moist northward moving maritime air coming from the Atlantic Ocean and the dry continental air coming from the African Landmass. The climate of the area is affected by ocean and atmospheric interactions both within and outside its environment, in which the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) plays a controlling factor. The movement of the ITCZ is associated with the warm humid maritime Tropical air mass with its south-western winds and the hot and dry continental air mass with its dry north-easterly winds.

**Local Climate**

The climate of project study area falls under the humid tropical rainy climates classified by Koppen and Geiger 1954 as Aw. The extent of rainfall periods ranges from 119 - 220 days, with the highest number recorded between the months of July and September, while the lowest is recorded during December and January. A brief dry spell is recorded between the months of August (August break), however, this is not usual as August within the last few years yields high amount of rainfall in the area when compared to peak rainfall periods. The mean annual rainfall for Lagos is above 1,600mm with dominant wind direction being south-westerly during the wet season and northwesterly during the main dry season. The mean daily minimum temperature is about 23°C and a mean daily maximum temperature of about 31°C. The mean monthly relative humidity of the area varies from 70 - 89 %. Microclimatic data were acquired for the study area for a period of 24 hours and thirty (30) years (1989-2019) long term data (macroclimatic data) retrieved from the Nigerian Meteorological Agency, Lagos and literature sources. During field work survey, a weather monitoring equipment (Kestrel) was used to monitor the weather parameters essentially the temperature, wind speed, humidity, barometric pressure. Necessary precautions were taken when selecting the sampling points for measurement and during the duration measurements were observed for the onsite measurements to ensure that the data obtained are representatives of the proposed project location.

Some of these precautions include setting up the weather monitoring equipment away from obstacles like buildings and tall vegetation, taking cognizance of the predominant wind direction, orienting the instrument so that the sun's radiation does not fall directly on the instrument during reading and positioning the weather monitoring equipment in an area representative of the study area's totality. a) Nitrogen Oxides
b) Sulphur Oxides
c) Volatile Organic Compound
d) Particulate Matters (PM2.5 and PM10)
e) Hydrogen sulphide
f) Carbon monoxide

As earlier mentioned, the weather parameters were measured with a digital Kestrel meter with various sensors for the meteorological components and a digital compass for the wind direction. The Criteria air pollutants which include NOx, SOx, CO etc. were measured with the aid of Multi-RAE and V-RAE gas analyzer while the particulate matters were measured with an Aeroqual 500 PM monitor.

**Cloud Cover**

Generally, the weather condition during the period of field observation was relatively clear and the average cloud cover was 6 Oktas. High amounts of cloud inhibit instability that enhances the dispersion of plumes and lower amounts promote atmospheric dispersion of plumes. Cloud cover is prominent during rainy periods and minimal during dry seasons which either allow or hinder the penetration of solar energy which increases/decreases heat during these periods.

**Atmospheric Pressure**

Atmospheric pressure at sea level measured during the period of field work was between 1011.3-1012.3hPa. An important characteristic of the atmosphere is its pressure as it often determines wind and weather pattern across an area. The normal range of the earth's air pressure is from 970mbar to 1050mbar. These differences are the results of low and high air pressure systems which are caused by unequal heating across surfaces. Low pressure areas are usually associated with high winds, warm air and atmospheric lifting. Because of this, lows normally produce clouds, precipitation and other bad weather such as tropical storms and cyclones. Unlike areas of low pressure, the absence of clouds means that areas prone to high pressure experience extremes in diurnal and seasonal temperatures since there are no clouds to black incoming solar radiation or trap outgoing long wave radiation.

Field study revealed that the average weather parameters monitored for the study environment showed that ambient temperature, relative humidity, wind speed/direction, atmospheric pressure were 33.9°C, 69.5%, 2.2 m/s, SW, 1011.8hPa respectively. The study area features a humid tropical wet climate that is typical of the coastal areas in Nigeria. There are two rainy seasons, with the heaviest rains falling from June to July and a weaker rainy season in September and October. There is a brief relatively dry spell in August and a dry spell from December to February. Average annual rainfall is greater than 1,600 mm and ambient temperature in the range 23oC to 31oC. Relative humidity is always high during rainy months due to the presence of cloud cover and slightly lower during the dry season. One of the important weather parameters (wind speed/direction) analyzed shows that the dominant wind direction is WSW, meaning that air emissions will be dispersed at the north-east and eastern directions of the study environment. During field sampling wind speed was generally low at night and moderate during the day meaning that emissions would disperse faster during the day than at night. Ambient air quality measured for observed pollutants indicated a very low concentrations and far below set limits. Noise levels monitored were moderate and within ambient standards, except for periods of increased human activities which increases the noise levels.

**4.9 Field Studies and Laboratory Analysis**

Field studies were carried out between 11th and 13th November, 2022 to collect relevant baseline data of the study area. The main objective of the field data acquisition was to establish the current biophysical, social and health status of the local environment status of the project through collection of air, soil, groundwater, surface water, planktons, and benthos and sediment samples including ambient air quality and noise measurement. The baseline data acquisition exercise was undertaken for dry season and was witnessed by officers from Lagos State Ministry of the Environment and Water Resource.

Samples collected were analyzed at the Environmental Laboratories Limited at 28 Apaola Street, off Aladelola Street, Ikosi-Ketu, Lagos and methodologies used for sample analyses were based on the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) and American Public Health Association (APHA).

**(i) Sample Collection and Handling**

The following sample collection and handling procedures were adopted, in accordance with international best practice:
*   Only standard field procedure and method approved by Federal Ministry of Environment (FMEnv) 1995 were adopted.
*   All sampling equipment were maintained in excellent condition, calibrated against international standards and adequate steps were taken to ensure that they function normally.
*   Only new and thoroughly washed, rinsed and sterilized sampling containers were used.
*   For heavy metal determination, sampling bottles were used and were rinsed with a solution of one part nitric acid to four-parts water, followed by copious amounts of distilled water.
*   Water samples for BOD5 determination were collected in black oxygen bottles and kept away from light source.
*   All parameters without holding time were measured in situ on the field.
*   Samples for other ex-situ parameters were preserved as soon as they were collected. Dissolved oxygen samples were fixed with Winkler's Reagents; oil and grease and heavy metal samples were acidified to a pH of about 2 with concentrated HNO3 respectively.
*   All samples were adequately labeled to preserve their identity. Label included sample reference code number, source, sampling date and time etc.
*   All samples were safely packed and transported to the laboratory base.

**(ii) Sample Preservation**

To avoid samples contamination and deterioration, sampling tools and containers were pre-sterilized. Other measures employed included pre-treating and preserving samples in transit in ice-cooled chests to the laboratory and stored at that temperature in refrigerators pending subsequent analysis. All parameters with short holding time such as pH and temperature were measured in situ, while samples for delayed analyses were preserved by refrigeration (for general water chemistry), pH adjustment or chemical pre-treatment (for heavy metals, Total Hydrocarbon, Dissolved Oxygen).

Dissolved Oxygen were acidified to a pH 2 with concentrated H2SO4 and HNO3 respectively and samples for microbiology and benthos were stored immediately in ice-cooled chest (at 4°C) in transit to the laboratory base where they were stored in refrigerators. **Sulphur Dioxide**

SO2 is a colourless gas with a sharp odour. It is produced from the burning of fossil fuels (coal and oil) and the smelting of mineral ores that contain sulfur. The main anthropogenic source of SO2 is the burning of sulfur-containing fossil fuels for domestic heating, power generation and motor vehicles. SO2 can affect the respiratory system and the functions of the lungs, and causes irritation of the eyes. Inflammation of the respiratory tract causes coughing, mucus secretion, aggravation of asthma and chronic bronchitis and makes people more prone to infections of the respiratory tract. **Carbon Monoxide**

Carbon dioxide is a colorless, odorless and tasteless but poisonous gas produced primarily by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels. It constitutes the single largest pollutant in the urban atmosphere due to its emissions from internal combustion engines. Atmospheric levels of carbon monoxide in urban areas show a positive correlation with the density of vehicular traffic, and a negative correlation with wind speed. It has a strong affinity towards the hemoglobin of the bloodstream making it a dangerous asphyxiant. The main sources of CO in the urban air are smoke and exhaust fumes of many devices burning coal, gas or oil. Carbon dioxide enters the blood stream and reacts with the hemoglobin in the blood to form carboxyl-hemoglobin. This reduces oxygen delivery to the body's organs (like the heart and brain) and tissues, a situation which gives rise to myocardial ischemia and chest pain when exercising or under increased stress. At extremely high levels, CO can cause death. People with heart diseases are particularly sensitive. Exposure to high levels is linked with impairment of vision, work capacity, learning ability and performance of difficult tasks.

Carbon dioxide measurement was taken using an in-situ non-integrated single gas carbon monoxide monitor (ToxiRAE Model PGM-1150). **Particulate Matter (PM)**

Particulate Matter is a general term used for a mixture of solid particles and liquid droplets in the air. It includes aerosols, smoke, fumes, dust, ash and pollen. These particulates are categorized according to sizes of the particles in microns such as PM1.0, PM2.5, PM7.0, and PM10. Particles of different sizes in air when inhaled have different health effects on man. PM2.5 and PM10 are considered most important due to their public health risks.

*   **PM2.5**: These are particulate matters which have aerodynamic diameter not exceeding 2.5microns. They are also referred to as fine or respirable particles because of their abilities to penetrate the respiratory system further than larger particles. Their sources are primarily from fossil fuel combustion, vegetation burning and smelting/processing of metals. The life span when released into the atmosphere ranges from days to weeks. Owing to the health effects of this pollutant, the World Health Organization (WHO) set standards of 25ug/m3 and 10µg/m3 for short- and long-term exposures respectively.
*   **PM10**: These are particles of sizes not exceeding the 10um in diameter (about one-seventh the diameter of a human hair). They are primarily produced during construction activities, demolition, quarry activities, roadside dust suspension and wind. These particulates, when inhaled, attach to small hairs (Cellia) in the nostrils tract and are subsequently released upon clearing of throat.

**Effects of PM2.5 and PM10**

Fine particles, especially those smaller than 10 microns in diameter (PM10), when inhaled into human lungs, can cause premature death, respiratory diseases such as asthma. These health effects can lead to increase in hospital bills, use of medications, days off school, and loss of man hour. Also, fine particulates have some environmental effects such as visibility impairment, acidification of lakes and streams, change in soil nutrients, destruction of sensitive forests and farm crops etc. The size of particles is directly linked to their potential for causing health problems. Small particles less than 10 micrometers in diameter pose the greatest problems, because they can get deep into lungs, and some may even get into bloodstream. Exposure to such particles can affect both the lungs and the heart. Numerous scientific studies have linked particle pollution exposure to a variety of problems, including:
*   Premature death in people with heart or lung diseases
*   Nonfatal heart attacks
*   Irregular heartbeat
*   Aggravated asthma
*   Decreased lung function
*   Increased respiratory symptoms, such as irritation of the airways, coughing or difficulty breathing.

People with heart or lung diseases, children, and older adults are the most likely to be affected by particle pollution exposure.

To measure the Particulate Matter, the Sensors are housed within an interchangeable 'sensor head' that attaches to the monitor base. Each sensor head contains a single gas or particle sensor. Each sensor head contains a fan that works continuously or periodically (depending on the sensor type) to draw sample air to the sensor. Once sampled, the air is exhausted from the opposite side of the sensor head.

**Ozone**

Ozone is a colorless, odorless gas at ambient concentrations and is a major component of smog. Ozone can be good or bad depending on where it occurs:
*   Ozone occurs naturally in the Earth's upper atmosphere (the stratosphere), where it shields the Earth from the sun's ultraviolet rays.
*   At ground-level, ozone is an air pollutant that can harm human health.

Ground-level ozone is not emitted directly into the atmosphere. It results from photochemical reactions between oxides of nitrogen (NOx) and Volatile Organic Compound (VOC) in the presence of sunlight. Studies have shown that high Ozone levels typically occur from May to September between noon and early evening. The reactions can be represented by the chemical equation:
NO2 + hv -> NO + O
O + O2 -> O3

They are also found in emissions from:
*   Vehicles such as automobiles, trucks, buses, aircraft, and locomotives
*   Construction equipment
*   Lawn and garden equipment
*   Sources that combust fuel, such as large industries and utilities
*   Small industries such as gas stations and print shops
*   Consumer products, including some paints and cleaners.

**Effects of Ozone.**

Ozone irritates the respiratory tract and eyes. Exposure to high levels of O3 results in chest tightness, coughing and wheezing. It makes the lung more susceptible to infection and aggravates lung diseases such as asthma, emphysema and chronic bronchitis thus making people with respiratory and heart problems at a higher risk. Ozone has been linked to increased hospital admissions and premature death. The recommended standards by the World Health Organization for the criteria pollutants are indicated in table 4.13.

Microorganism identification was carried out by the conventional process. **Soil Microbiology**

Microbes in the soil have substantial role in the recycling of nutrients, and break down of dead and decomposing organic matter and in the aiding the attenuation of soil contaminants. Heterotrophic bacterial derive their carbon and energy from complex organic substance/organic matter.

*   **Total Heterotrophic Bacterial (THB) Count**: A hundred-fold serial dilutions of the water samples were carried out five times in a set of test tubes containing 9.9 ml sterile distilled water (Dilutions 10-2, 10-5, 10-6, 10-8, and 10-10). 1 ml of each dilution was then plated using nutrient agar medium (sterile) kept in molten form. Pour plate method was thereafter adopted in inoculation. Having allowed the agar plates to set, the culture plates were incubated aerobically at 35°C for 36-48 hours, thus only allowing the enumeration of aerobes and facultative heterotrophic bacteria. The plates were observed for growth and selected for count after the expiration of the incubation period. The culture plate in which the number of colonies was less than 300 was selected for each sample. The count was multiplied by the dilution factor and expressed as colony forming unit (cfu) per milliliter of the original sample. This represented a viable count, i.e. Plate Count (cfu/ml) = (No. of Colonies on Plate) x (Dilution Factor).
*   **Total Heterotrophic Fungi (THF) Count**: A similar procedure for THB was repeated for the total fungi count. The differences are as follows:
    *   Malt extract agar was employed as the culture medium.
    *   The culture plates were incubated aerobically at 30°C for 5 - 7 days (until the plates showed no further increase in the number of fungal colonies).

**Isolation and Identification of Microorganisms**

Bacteria and fungi isolates were obtained from the previous culture plates and tubes. Parameters used in differentiating each isolate included colonial characteristics (elevation, edges, texture, pigmentation etc); cell morphology (shape, arrangement and gram reaction); and spore formation. The bacteria isolates were sub cultured on nutrient agar slants while the fungi were sub cultured on malt extract agar slants for further work. Primary identification for each bacterial isolated was carried out by sub-culturing onto eosin methylene blue agar, MacConkey agar; triple sugars iron agar for differentiating enteric bacilli; mannitol salt agar for staphylococci; blood agar for haemolytic cocci and nutrient agar for other bacteria. Further and confirmatory biochemical reactions in the identification processes were carried out employing various tests such as gelatin liquefaction, nitrate reduction, sugars fermentation, indole formation, H2S production and measurements of motility, citrate utilization, catalase, coagulase, MRVP and oxidase.

The information generated /data collected for the various isolates was compared with standards available in various manuals and textbooks such as the Bergy's Manual of Determinative Bacteriology (Bread et al., 1962). Moulds and yeast isolates were identified by their growth characteristics (i.e. texture, pigmentation, form, spore formation etc.) and morphology. Staining techniques using lacto-phenol in cotton blue (for moulds) and gram staining (for yeasts) were used. The prepared slides were examined afterwards under the microscope. Drawings of the various structures (i.e. sporangium, sporangiophores, collumellae, mycelium, spores, ascospores, etc.) of each isolate were made, studied and compared with the standard drawings of commonly encountered moulds and yeasts in manual.

**Community Structure Analysis**

Biodiversity or Biological Diversity is the sum of all the different species of animals, plants, fungi, and microbial organisms living on Earth and the variety of habitats in which they live. Biodiversity is also the variation of life forms within a given ecosystem, biome or for the entire Earth. Consequently, biodiversity is often used as a measure of the health of systems. A diversity index is a mathematical measure of species diversity in a community. Diversity indices provide important information about rarity and commonness of species in a community. The ability to quantify diversity in this way is an important tool for biologists trying to understand community structure. The following diversity indices were used for biological data:

*   **Species Richness Index (d)**: The Species richness index (d) according to Margalef (1951) is a measure of diversity and was used to evaluate the community structure. Species Richness is a measure of the number of different kinds of organisms present in a particular area. This index is also referred to as Margalef index.
*   **Menhinick's Index (D)** (Ogbeibu, 2005): The Menhinick's Index (D) is one of several diversity indices used to quantify diversity and hence measure diversity in categorical data. It represents a biological association with a number that give a measure of its community structure.
*   **Shannon and Weiner diversity index (Hs)** (Ogbeibu, 2005): The Shannon and Weiner diversity index (Hs) is one of several diversity indices used to measure diversity in categorical data. It is simply the Information entropy of the distribution, treating species as symbols and their relative population sizes as the probability. Shannon and Wiener (1963) diversity index is also called Shannon index.
*   **Species Equitability or Evenness index (j)** (Ogbeibu, 2005): The Species Equitability or Evenness index (j) is one of several diversity indices used to measure diversity in categorical data. Evenness is a measure of the relative abundance of the different species making up the richness of an area.
*   **Simpsons Dominance index (C)** (Ogbeibu, 2005): Simpson's Diversity Index is a measure of diversity. In ecology, it is often used to quantify the biodiversity of a habitat. It considers the number of species present, as well as the abundance of each species. Simpson's diversity index (D) is a simple mathematical measure that characterizes species diversity in a community. The proportion of species i relative to the total number of species (pi) are calculated and squared. The squared proportions for all the species are summed, and the reciprocal is taken.

**4.12 Land Use Sensitivity**

Adequate and comprehensive data on the land uses of an area can only be acquired only if the data from the both the space, atmosphere and ground surfaces are integrated with those obtained from a number of other collateral sources. Based on the reconnaissance survey carried out in the project area, the land uses within the immediate vicinity of the project site is residential with few other uses like commercial and transportation. These developments among others include residential building, schools, shops, bus stops etc. The nature of the some of the building structures in the area is discussed in the later section of this chapter on existing social infrastructure of the project area.

The proposed site is situated along the Lagos Lagoon and there are no sites of historical or cultural importance within the proposed site. Therefore, the project implementation shall have no impact on any archaeological or cultural heritage aspect of the environment. The immediate environment of the project site as presently constituted is a mixed land use development.

**Vegetation**

Vegetation of an area constitutes the totality of the plants in that area. Vegetation is one of the most important expression of the health of an environment as it optimizes climatic and edaphic conditions. Vegetation further reflects the level of human interference with nature and its state in many situation, reflects of human being, regulation of water quality and balance in soil and air; as well as playing a role in climate. Generally, vegetation performs several functions that are crucial to the sustenance of the environment. This includes:
*   Protection of the fragile soils from the erosive impacts of rains and wind;
*   Maintenance of soil fertility through continuous nutrient recycling;
*   Conservation of water resources through shading;
*   Preservation of water sheds;
*   Regulation of air and soil temperature;
*   Moisture balance;
*   Provision of habitat for countless terrestrial flora and fauna;
*   Purification of the environment through carbon dioxide during photosynthesis and the release of oxygen for human and animal respiration.

Lagos is characterized by coastal environment (sandy barrier beaches, low-lying tidal flats, and estuaries) with three vegetation types (Mangrove, Secondary Rain Forest and Freshwater Swamp). The flora diversity of the project area and field collection and identification of plant species occurring within the study area was conducted. The occurrence of plant species based on the studied location were recorded. The collected plant species were identified by expert in plant taxonomy. The correct names of the identified species followed International Plant Name Index (IPNI). A comprehensive list of species was thereafter carefully documented, along with their families, habitats and local names by which they are known within the study area.

Ethnobotanical information about the identified plant species was obtained through oral interviews. The interviews were conducted randomly with a total of eleven (11) local respondents including herb sellers, herbalists, elderly people and others with knowledge of herbal medicine. The consent of all the respondents was sought before the interviews. The interviews were held in local language and dialects such as Yoruba, Awori and Egun; hence, there was no need for interpreters and this allowed accurate data recording. The information collected included the local names, ethno botanical uses/the parts used, modes of preparation and administration of the identified plants. The proposed project site is along the Lagos Lagoon waterfront adjoined by highly built up areas therefore characterised by spare secondary vegetation.

**4.12.1 Wildlife and Endangered Species**

Interviews with the residents of the project area revealed that a process of progressive extinction of larger and valuable wildlife species has occurred for several decades. Most of the wildlife has been pushed further into more remote parts of the surrounding forest in the project area due to many of the anthropogenic activities which are largely connected to development. Nevertheless, field observations were carried out both within the proposed project site and other vegetation sampling locations to obtain information on the animal and wildlife resources within the study area. The well branched trees of the project site are habitats to avifauna species, crawling reptiles, arboreal primates, arthropods, molluscs and small animals. The wildlife species prevailing in the area for the wet season varied from invertebrates to large reptiles, birds, and small mammals. Generally, the invertebrate groups consist of several arthropod groups including butterflies, moths, dragon flies, water boatman, beetle, praying mantes, spiders, ants and termites. The mammalian groups include giant rats, gazelles, porcupines, ant eaters, bats.

The rest of the animals moved for safety except the aquatic wetland birds that closely associated with water bodies due to their feeding, breeding, roosting behavior and other associated activities with aquatic environment. The common species of the wetland birds found are herons, waterfowl, fish eagle and daters, the majority of which are piscivorous.

**Wildlife Diversity**

From the results obtained, it was found that the area harbour an impressive array of mammalian species in spite of its highly perturbed nature. Information from local hunters obtained show a sharp drop in the number of game animals they were able to kill recently as compared to the past.

The abundance of wildlife in the bush meat market during the study season was far lesser than what was observed in the past. This could be attributed to the fact that the flood reduced in the habitable space of most animals. Thus in the little space left, they clustered and became the more available for hunters to shoot unlike in the past, when the wetland is enormous, there is more space available for games, so the animals disperse far apart and therefore become less available for hunters.

The terrestrial wildlife of the region consists of birds, reptiles, amphibians and invertebrates. They include the rodent (Rodentia), primates (Primates), Pangolins (Pholidota), Carnivores (Carnivora), insectivores (Insectivora) and ungulates (Artiodactyla). The rodents were the most dominant group, constituting about 50% of the total number of mammalian species recorded. Except for the Giant rats the rodents are small mammals and are very varied in pelage coloration and patterning. They are mostly terrestrial and live in burrows, being mostly nocturnal. Because of the large numbers they are neither threatened nor endangered but rather considered a pest to field crops and stored products. The reptiles were represented by chameleons, geckos, monitor lizards, agama lizards, snakes and skinks. Apart from the snakes most reptilian species are neither endangered nor threatened.

Terrestrial and soil invertebrate fauna present were observed. The dense undergrowths and fertile soils harboured earthworms, millipedes, centipedes, spiders, mites and ticks, daddy longleg, woodlice, butterflies and moths, short horn and long horn grasshoppers, cockroaches, bees, aphids, longicorn and ground beetles, leaf and ladybird beetles, leaf hoppers, dragon and damsel flies, ants, midge, housefly, blowfly, mosquitoes, black fly, deer fly, mayfly, stick insect, caddisflies, bristleflies, crickets, mantids, various bugs, wasps, hornets, termites, snails and slugs.

The population of the project host community was projected using results from 1991 and 2006 national censuses releases by the national population commission (NPC). Two mathematical population projection methods (the linear extrapolation and exponential growth models) are often used in estimating the population of area of interest. The extrapolation model assumes that population growth occurs in constant increments over time while the exponential model assumes that population growth occurs in constant increments over time but rather changes with time, growing faster as the population size increases. In other words population often tends to grow exponentially rather than linearly. Based on this, the exponential growth model was used in estimating the population of the communities.

The formula is:
Exponential growth model
Pn = Po(1+r)^n
Where:
Po = population in the base year (in this case 2006)
r = annual growth rate of the population (as obtained from NPC and Lagos State)
n = time lapse, in years

Although a national census as conducted in 2006, figures for the individual settlements and localities are yet to be published. Results for the 36 states and the Federal Capital Territory, Abuja, and the 776 Local Government Council areas (LGAs) are the only available records. As a consequence, the population of the project - affected community was projected using the 1991/96 and 2006 (for affected LGAs only) population figures, augmented with secondary information.

**4.16 Community Health Environment and Related Issues**

**Water Supply**

Access to portable water is one of the Sustainable Development Goals and is measured by the percentage of the population with safe drinking water in their dwelling or located within a convenient distance from the user's dwelling (UN 1996). According to the United Nations, safe water includes treated surface waters and untreated but uncontaminated water such as that from protected boreholes, springs and sanitary wells. The community has no public water supply as most of the residents obtain water supply from well and borehole systems dug within their premises.

Finding during the FGD revealed that water from these two facilities are not drinkable as most premises owner cannot afford the required expensive treatment process that can make the water safe for drinking. Water from these facilities are usually used for cooking, washing and other domestic related activities. The well water systems in most cases are located in an open spaces in front of the premises with an unrestricted access to the facility by neighbours. The residents claimed to rely on sachet water and other treated bottled like water for drinking purposes within the community.

**Health Care Facilities, Morbidity Patterns and Health Management:**

Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social wellbeing, without any form of infirmity. In examining development in the health domain, the main issues vital to the health of the citizenry include access of the people to social and health facilities, availability of health facilities, availability of health care services, survival chances and the issue of reproductive health. The measurement of access to health care services is based on the number of health facilities per 1000 people. In other words, accessibility to health care services is conceptualized on the number of health and service centers (primary, secondary and tertiary) available per 1000 people. The life expectancy estimates for Lagos State is the same as the national estimate.

The world Health Organization (W.H.O) in its World Health Statistics 2006 estimated that life expectancy for men in Nigeria are 42 years and 47 years for women. Critically, fertility of the populace is important and is best measured by the Total Fertility Rate (TFR), which is an indication of the total number of children a woman is estimated to have in her productive lifetime. Existence of precise estimates of the TFR values for this L.G.A could not be ascertained as no values were available, however, the National Bureau of Statistics estimates the value for the South-West Geo Political Zone as 4.6 while the national average is 5.25 children born / woman. **Basic Sanitation / Waste Management**

According to the WHO, sanitation is defined as facilities and services for the safe disposal of human urine and faeces. The synergy between water and sanitation stems from the fact that both work together to reduce the pathogen load in the ambient environment, and in the interruption of the transmission of the pathogens. The accumulation of waste products and their indiscriminate disposal represent a grave hazard to health of the general populace. Systems of waste disposals are designed to eliminate these hazards. Access to basic sanitation is conceptualized on the proportion of the total population with access to sanitary facilities for human waste disposal in the dwelling or immediate vicinity. Ideally, there should be at least one latrine for each family, and the facility should always be kept clean. The environment is well maintained and attractive. Most parts of the estate are well landscaped and there are green areas in front of houses.

Most homes within the study area have good toilet facilities. Personal water closet was the commonest method (99.9%) for sewage disposal in the households. Generally, observation show that refuse disposal practices is fair. Waste management in the study area is handled by private waste management concessioner approved by the Lagos state Waste Management Authority. According to the study by Obianuju B, et al (2018), Lagos produces 13 million Kg of solid wastes every day without adequate solid waste handling facilities.

**Housing In the Project Area**

The provision of good housing is an important aspect of environmental health as it represents a significant part of man's environment. The project area is rural community with most houses still representing houses expected to see in rural areas. The W.H.O reports that in 2004, there were 5.0 deaths hospital beds per 10,000 people (W.H.O 2011). Nigeria's crude birth rate in 2009 was 40 per 1,000 people. **4.17 Public Perception, Concerns of the Public about the Project**

The local community was keen to talk to the EIA field team on the proposed project and they were appreciative of the fact that the field team engaged them in Focus Group Discussion and also involved them in responding to the questionnaire in a consultative manner. The people encountered participated actively in raising their concerns and they expressed their hope that lawful procedures will be taken into consideration during the project implementation. The household respondents expressed various opinions about the proposed Container Terminal development project with more than half making favourable comments. The household respondents had more favourable opinions than the Key Informants. The reasons adduced by those in support of the project includes the following:
i. The proposed project will lead to further development of the community and the project area
ii. The proposed project will create employment opportunities and improvement in the socio-economic life of the people of the community.
iii. The project will boost businesses and reduce social vises as some idled hands will be employed especially during the site clearing and construction phase

However, one-fifth of the respondents had negative opinion about the project. These categories of respondents expressed dissenting views about the project as follows:
*   The project may encourage traffic bottleneck around the project area particularly along the Lagos-Badagry Expressway due to movement of cars and trucks supply goods from the Badagry Deep sea port in/out of the estate premises;
*   The project construction phase may increase STD infection in the project area due to influx of people seeking to patronize the company handling the construction work
*   The project may contribute to excessive consumption of resources such as energy, water and thus contributing to emission of greenhouse gases to the project area.
*   The project may lead to increase in rent and general prices of commodities and services. Although, this is debatable whether this is generally positive or negative. For instance, the landlords would probably be happy with increase in rent while tenants will be unhappy about the same
*   The project could lead to increase in air-borne diseases arising from the construction works
*   Increased in noise impact due to the use of construction machinery and equipment
*   Owners of some make shift shops located on the setback of Lagos-Badagry Highway in front of the proposed project site also expressed concerns that the project development will lead to demolition of their shops and that the proponent may count them worthy of compensation of loss of their means of livelihood

However, the stakeholders proposed some measures to mitigate their concerns on the project and this includes the following:
*   The proponent should show sincerity of purpose, openness, dialogue and carry all concerned stakeholders along in the course of implementing the project as this is expected to bring the foundation for a good proponent- community relationship.
*   Provision of employment and youth empowerment for people of the project area
*   Effective monitoring of waste reduction of environmental nuisances that may be created during the construction and residence/operation phase of the project.
